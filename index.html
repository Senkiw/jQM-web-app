Step 1 of 7

Species

There are over 100 coffee species in the world, however there are two main species that are widely sold and procuded. These two main species of coffee are: Arabica and Robusta.

Arabica Beans

Also know as Coffee Arabica. Thought to be the very first species of coffee to be cultivated, over 1,000 years ago.

It has:

  • Less caffeine
  • Less aciditiy
  • More aromatic properties
  • and are considered the more superior bean

Robusta Beans

Also known as Coffea Canephora. Originating in the Congo Basin, this species is widely grown in Arfica and Asia.

It has:

  • A strong, full-body
  • Distinctive earthy flavour
  • More bitterness
  • and is grown in a climate that is unsuitable for Arabica beans


From here on we will focus on the more popular, Arabica beans.

Step 3 of 7

Processing

"Processing" describes how the beans are removed from the coffee cherry. Processing plays a part in overall flavour, acidity, and body.

Washed Processing

Light, clean and tangy

  • Pulp is mechanically removed, leaving a sticky layer called mucilage on the beans
  • Beans are washed and held in a concrete fermentation tank for 18-36 hours to develop their flavour, acidity, and body
  • One final washing, and the beans are dried, parchment is removed and the beans are sorted

Semi-Washed Processing

Lower acidity, light-bodied and smooth

  • Pulp is removed using small hand-pulping machines, and then the coffee is washed and laid out in the sun
  • After partial drying, parchment is removed and the coffee is laid out again for final drying
  • The beans are then sorted and graded

Natural Processing

Wild, unpredictable and berry

  • Coffee cherries dry completely on the bean
  • This develops flavours before the skin and parchment are removed
  • This is followed by sorting and grading on the beans

Step 4 of 7

Roasting

There are three general catagories for roasting: blonde, medium, and dark.

Shorter roast time, lighter body, mellow flavours.

Balanced, with smooth and rich flavours.

Longest roast time, fuller body and robust, bold flavours.

Aroma

The way the coffee smells - it could be earthy, spicy, floral, nutty or others. These distinctive aromas are directly related to the coffee's flavour.

Acidity

The tangy or tartness quality, not the actual pH level of the coffee. You feel it on the sides and tip of your tongue, the way you do when you taste citrus fruits or drinks. Coffee with high acidity is described as bright, tangy and crisp with a clean finish. Low-acidity coffee feels smooth in your mouth and lingers.

Body

The weight of the coffee on your tongue. Like comparing whole milk with nonfat milk. Light-bodied coffees feel light and don't linger; you feel the weight of full-bodied coffees on your tongue and the flavour lingers.

Flavour

The way the coffee tastes. Citus, cocoa and berries are just a few flavours you might taste. Some are obvious, others are subtle.

Step 7 of 7

Tasting Steps

Follow each of these steps each time you tasste to truly appreciate the characteristics of the coffees. See how the characteristics you like correspond to region, processing method and roast.

  1. Smell: Always smell before you taste. Your tongue only distinguishes four tastes - but your nose can detect many more smells. Cup you hand over the coffee, hold close to your nose and inhale.
  2. Slurp: Slurp, don't sip your coffee. It allows the coffee to spray across your palate and lets subtle flavours and aromas reach your nose.
  3. Locate: Think about where the flavours hit you on your tongue. Is it the side or the tip of your tongue? What does the weight feel like and where?
  4. Describe: Now you have smelled and tasted the coffee think about the experience. How would you describe it? Describe the aroma, acidity, body, and flavour. Think of foods that would pair well.
